AI tools I actually use vs the ones that are overhyped

After a year of trying every AI tool that crossed my LinkedIn feed, here's what actually stuck in my workflow — and what turned out to be mostly marketing.
Read more ↓

Let me be honest: I got caught up in the AI hype like everyone else. Every week there was a new tool that was supposed to "revolutionize" something. I signed up for most of them.

A year later, here's what I actually use daily: ChatGPT for drafting, brainstorming, and rubber-ducking problems. Claude when I need longer, more structured thinking. Excel Copilot — honestly a mixed bag, sometimes helpful, sometimes makes things worse.

What turned out to be overhyped (for me at least): most "AI-powered project management" tools. They add AI labels to features that are basically search and auto-fill. The dashboards look fancy in demos but I still end up building my own in Power BI because I need specific things.

The real shift wasn't any single tool. It was learning to think in terms of prompts and structured outputs. Once I understood that, even basic tools became more useful. The expensive enterprise AI tools? Most of them solve problems you can solve with a well-written prompt and a spreadsheet.

This isn't a hot take — it's what I've seen working across 4 companies now. The teams that benefit from AI aren't the ones with the fanciest tools. They're the ones with clean data and clear processes to begin with.

What I learned switching from engineering to business

I studied mechanical engineering. Now I work in digital transformation and product strategy. The transition wasn't as dramatic as it sounds — but some things surprised me.
Read more ↓

People keep asking me: "Why did you switch from engineering?" The honest answer is that I didn't really switch. I just moved from one end of the building process to another.

In engineering, you learn to break complex problems into smaller pieces, test assumptions, and iterate. That's literally what product management and project coordination are. The vocabulary changed. The thinking didn't.

What surprised me was how much of business runs on spreadsheets and presentations. I expected sophisticated systems. What I found was that the person who could build a clean Excel model and present it clearly had more influence than the person with the fanciest MBA framework.

The hardest part wasn't learning new tools — it was learning to be comfortable with ambiguity. Engineering has right answers. Business has "good enough for now, let's test it" answers. That took some getting used to.

My advice for other engineers thinking about this move: your analytical thinking is your superpower. Don't try to become a different person. Just apply the same rigour to different problems. And learn Excel. Seriously. Learn it well.
